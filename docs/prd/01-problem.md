# The Problem

## What We're Solving

Urban farming has enormous potential — it reduces food miles, builds community resilience, lowers household food costs, and reclaims underused urban land. But most people who could participate never start, and most who start don't sustain it.

The reasons are consistent:

**High barrier to entry.** New farmers don't know what to grow, when to grow it, or how to manage soil conditions. Without guidance, early failures are common and discouraging.

**Isolation.** Individual urban farms operate alone. There is no mechanism for coordination, so everyone tends to grow the same popular crops, creating local oversupply in some areas and gaps in others.

**Waste.** Without coordination, excess produce has nowhere to go. A single household rarely needs everything they grow, but there's no easy channel to share or sell surplus.

**No comparative advantage.** Every farmer tries to grow everything. But some plots are better suited for leafy greens, others for root vegetables, others for herbs. Without a system that recognizes and routes this, the whole network underperforms.

**Cost and sustainability.** Uncoordinated urban farming is often more expensive and wasteful than it needs to be — wrong inputs, wrong timing, wrong crops for the conditions.

---

## Why This Matters

Urban food insecurity is a growing problem. At the same time, cities have significant latent capacity — balconies, rooftops, community lots, backyards — that sits unused. The gap is not land or people, it is **coordination and knowledge**.

A well-coordinated urban farming network could function as a distributed, low-cost, highly sustainable grocery system. The food is grown where it's consumed, waste is minimized, and the cost of entry drops close to zero when the network is doing the thinking for you.

This is the core bet: **collective intelligence applied to urban farming produces better outcomes than any individual farmer acting alone.**

## Stakeholders

### New Urban Farmer
**What they care about:** Not failing. Low cost to start. Clear, simple instructions. Confidence that their effort will produce results.

**How MyCelium aligns:** The network tells you exactly what to grow based on your specific conditions and what the community needs. You don't need to know what you're doing — the system does.

### Established Urban Farmer
**What they care about:** Maximizing yield, minimizing waste, earning something for their surplus.

**How MyCelium aligns:** Optimized growing instructions per cycle. A marketplace to sell or exchange surplus at fair value. Digital currency that rewards contribution.

### Food-Insecure Community Member
**What they care about:** Access to affordable, fresh food nearby.

**How MyCelium aligns:** Network-produced food is distributed through local hubs at low cost. The currency system keeps prices accessible while still rewarding farmers.

### The Network (System Level)
**What it cares about:** Diverse, complete food supply across the community. No single point of failure. Continuous improvement as more nodes join.

**How MyCelium aligns:** The optimization algorithm routes production toward comparative advantage. The more nodes join, the more complete and resilient the supply.

## Key Constraints

| Constraint | Implication |
|-----------|-------------|
| Users may have very limited farming knowledge | Instructions must be simple, actionable, and specific |
| Urban plots are small and highly variable | Optimization must work at fine granularity (a balcony is a node) |
| Connectivity may be intermittent | Data sync must be tolerant of gaps; instructions must be self-contained |
| Trust is fragile in a new community | Currency and abuse prevention must be robust from day one |
| The network has zero value with zero nodes | Launch strategy must create a critical mass quickly in a focused geography |
| Comparative advantage only works at scale | Early network may need to run simplified rules before full optimization is viable |
